About me

Nice to meet you — I'm Ylyas

I'm a 23-year-old software engineer and entrepreneur from Turkmenistan, currently living in Malaysia. I build across the entire stack, but I’ve always gravitated toward the heavy lifting most people skip: designing complex databases, locking down access control, and making sure infrastructure doesn't collapse when things go completely wrong in production.

My story

Who am i?

A resume tells you what tools I can use. This section is about how I think, how I handle pressure, and what drives me to build.

01

Learning to execute from absolute zero

I learned what it means to build things by watching my father. He’s been an entrepreneur his entire life, starting at just 17 years old. He began with absolutely nothing—selling homemade milk on the streets—and fought his way up to establishing one of the top construction companies in Turkmenistan. He didn’t have a business degree or a manual when he started; he just executed, learned from every mistake, and adapted on the fly.

After high school, I stepped into that world for a year to test myself. Working inside his construction company, I spent every single day with a mentor who forced me into tough environments early on. He taught me the underlying human side of business—how to talk to people, master communication skills, and ensure a critical message actually lands when you deliver it.

Within four months, I went from just listening to actively operating. I was handling government licensing, bidding on public tenders, navigating sales negotiations, and helping with the corporate hiring process. Sitting in the main boardroom meetings, I spent hours analyzing how people communicated—how often they spoke, when they chose to hold back, and how they shifted the room. I realized very quickly that the most powerful leverage in business isn’t speaking the loudest; it’s being genuinely interested in the person across from you and actually listening to what they need.

02

My first business

Once I understood how government legalities and corporate frameworks operated, my mentor and I spotted a massive, painful problem in our local market. Thousands of aspiring founders desperately wanted to launch startups, secure patents, or legalize their companies, but they were completely paralyzed by the bureaucratic nightmare. They had no idea which department to visit, which documents to file, or how to navigate the paperwork. Most gave up before they even started.

We built a solution for them. We went directly to each major government department and negotiated streamlined, back-end channels to process corporate registrations from scratch. We turned a multi-month administrative headache into a sleek, 2-to-3-week service, and we charged good money for it because we were solving a massive frustration.

After our first two clients, word spread fast. Our phones wouldn't stop ringing, and our operation scaled 3x to 5x almost overnight. The volume grew so fast that I brought in my cousin and a crew of his friends to help manage our document pipelines. These guys were 7 to 10 years older than me, but they were jobless at the time and saw the incredible momentum we had created. For six months, we ran that niche, expanded our services, and became well-known. That was the moment I realized that the instinct to build, automate, and solve market problems was permanently in my blood.

03

Going further in life

I had a highly profitable business running smoothly in my home country, but I refused to let myself get comfortable. I was young, and I knew that if I stayed where things were easy, my growth would stop. I wanted to see the world, explore untouched environments, and live a completely independent life abroad.

There is a massive mental shift that happens when you pack your life into bags and move halfway across the world alone. When you physically widen your horizon, your entire cognitive engine expands with it. Your ideas grow larger, your problem-solving adapts to global dimensions, and your analytical skills sharpen because you no longer have a safety net. That is exactly why I left the day-to-day operations of my business behind and moved to Malaysia to pursue my computer science degree at Swinburne University.

When traditional recruiters or tech companies look at my background, they tend to view things through a static lens: “Not enough years of experience in this specific niche.” “Doesn’t know this exact framework on our checklist.”

They are looking at the world through a rearview mirror. We live in an era where the technical ground shifts beneath our feet daily. New chips are manufactured, new AI models drop overnight, and entirely new frameworks disrupt the market in months. In a world moving this fast, hiring for a static skill set is a failing strategy. You shouldn't look for a fixed asset; you should look for raw velocity and adaptability.

04

Getting comfortable with being uncomfortable

Throughout my entire four-year degree, I was constantly thrown into high-pressure projects where I had zero baseline knowledge. I was forced to handle complex stakeholder requirements, conduct market research, and understand deep software problems from a strict business perspective. I had to figure out how to solve problems people actually faced—or better yet, engineer solutions to problems the market didn't even realize they had until the product was sitting right in front of them.

Because technology moves too fast for a university syllabus to keep up, I spent my entire degree continuously self-teaching. My eyes were constantly on fire for the unknown—driven by an intense hunger to learn a framework I’d never heard of, implement it, and seamlessly merge it with existing technologies. You can train anyone to write syntax, but you cannot code human resourcefulness and critical intuition. That is what separates a true builder from an AI model, and it's the exact characteristic businesses need to survive.

The speed I bring to my technical engineering is directly fueled by the intense discipline I demand from my physical life. Living entirely on my own, I took total ownership of my health—completely rebuilding my physique through high-intensity weightlifting, running, swimming, and meticulous nutrition tracking.

Then in 2025, I stepped into a boxing gym, and my relationship with discomfort changed forever. Weightlifting is predictable; boxing is unyielding chaos. It drags you straight to your absolute physical limit within minutes and forces you to think rationally, analyze your opponent, and make logical decisions while your lungs are suffocating.

The real test of this mindset came during grueling 6-to-10-kilometer elevation hill runs. As someone who wasn't a natural runner, every single step was pure psychological warfare. When my shins, quads, and lungs were begging me to stop and lie down, I would force myself to ask simple questions: Are my legs physically broken? Has my body completely collapsed? No. I was still entirely capable of moving. Pushing past that imaginary mental barrier in the ring or on a steep hill is the exact same loop I use when debugging a complex system, tracing a network failure, or deploying an architecture under a crushing deadline.

"The limit is never physical. The limit is a lie your mind tells you because it wants to stay comfortable. Pushing past that point — in the gym, in the ring, or in a codebase — is the only place where growth actually happens." — the lesson I carry into everything I build
